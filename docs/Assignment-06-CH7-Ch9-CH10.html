The Bayesian updating calculation has a particularly simple mathematical form when the prior probability is stated in terms of odds.

\[\text{posterior odds} = \text{prior odds} \times \text{likelihood ratio} .\]

For the character who claims that the prior odds of Doom is zero, the posterior odds must always remain zero regardless of the likelihood ratio. In the movies, such a character always ends up dying in a dramatic way, just punishment for his skepticism.

Exercise 3 Prior and posterior: the ICBM-style example (from class and the book)

This exercise revisits the example from class and the book in which we learn an unknown probability of success (p) by starting with a prior distribution and updating with data to get a posterior distribution.

We use a uniform prior: before seeing any data, we treat every value of (p) between 0 and 1 as equally plausible. When we observe (k) successes in (n) trials, the mathematics of Bayesian updating (with this prior) gives a posterior that is a Beta distribution with shape parameters (= k + 1) and (= n - k + 1). The posterior density is highest where the data suggest (p) is likely to be. The MLE (maximum likelihood estimate) is (k/n), the fraction of successes—often shown as a vertical line on the plot.

Part 1: Flat prior, then 4 consecutive successes

Run the R chunk below. It defines a small function that plots the prior or posterior density of (p), then runs it for no data (prior) and for 1, 2, 3, and 4 successes in 1, 2, 3, and 4 trials (so four posteriors in a row). You get five plots: first the flat prior, then the posterior after 1 success in 1 try, then 2 in 2, then 3 in 3, then 4 in 4. The red dashed line in each posterior plot is the MLE ((k/n)).

Part 2: Informative prior from past data, then 4 new successes

Sometimes we already have past data. Suppose we had 7 successes in 16 trials and we use that to form our prior (so the prior is Beta(8, 10), no longer flat). Then we run 4 more trials and get 4 successes (11 successes in 20 trials total). The next chunk shows the prior (based on 7/16) and the posterior after updating with the new data (11/20).
