Exercise 4 A new dynamic system: predator and prey

In the SIR model, the state (susceptibles, infectives) moves in one general direction over time. Many two-variable dynamical systems behave differently. Here we introduce a simple predator–prey model (often called Lotka–Volterra). The state is \((R, F)\): \(R\) = prey population and \(F\) = predator population (e.g. in units per square km, or hundreds of individuals).

  • When there are many prey and few predators, prey tend to increase and predators can grow (more food).
  • When there are many predators and few prey, predators decline (not enough food) and prey can recover.
  • The dynamical rule has both \(R\) and \(F\) increasing or decreasing depending on the current state, and trajectories often cycle around an equilibrium instead of heading one way.

The figure below shows the flow field (dynamical rule) for such a model, with axes in population units. The domain extends toward the axes (low \(R\) or low \(F\)) so you can see how the flow behaves near those edge cases. Each arrow shows the direction and relative rate of change of the state. Three trajectories are drawn (green, brown, blue), starting at different states. Time runs along each curve; one full cycle might represent several years in a real system.

  1. How does this predator–prey system differ from the SIR epidemic model in terms of long-term behavior?
leh-vs-SIR

SIR has no state space; predator-prey does.

In SIR the state eventually moves in one general direction (e.g. toward fewer infectives); in this predator-prey model the state can cycle and not settle at a single point.

SIR has two state variables; predator-prey has only one.

Predator-prey has no equilibrium; SIR always has one.
